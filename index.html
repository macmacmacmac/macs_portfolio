Catalog DNA LLM Project

Catalog DNA is a biotech startup developing DNA base computing and storage technologies. One problem with almost all R&D is the vast volumes of natural language data that needs to be parsed---SOTA published literature, old notes from members no longer on the project, experiment logs lost to archives with outdated formats, etc. Filtering and parsing all these documents takes researchers hundreds of hours which incurs a massive financial cost. 

As a solution: I implemented a retrieval-augmented generation system leveraging LLMs and vector embeddings to index these vast natural language data stores which is able to query data stores up to terabytes in size efficiently. This led to significantly more efficient indexing and querying of natural language research data. I gained a lot of experience not just in language modeling over the course of the project, but also in all the data engineering tasks necessary to process and query the data as well as in web app development and model deployment for the user-facing side.

WPI DL for Neuroimaging

I worked on a project at the WPI neuroscience lab applying SOTA CNN techniques in neuroimaging domain for the purpose of identifying significant predictors of depression and anxiety.

We submitted the project proposal to NIH, after which we received access to their 21,000 subjects dataset containing brain scans of adolescents taken across a three year study period, totaling close to 40,000 neuroimages in total. As a result of the volume of data, I had to collaborate with my project partner to design and implement a data preprocessing pipeline to handle this volume.

The project is on-going and we are currently in the process of experimenting with data augmentation techniques to improve the network's performance.  

Microplastics Sentiment Research

There are many studies which asks "how aware are people of microplastics?"What is scarcely studied, however, is "how aware do people think others are of microplastics?"

This question is extremely important---people make decisions not only on their own but based on their prediction of the decisions of others. Reforms that require collective action in particular are highly susceptible. (Nobody goes to a protest if they don't believe others will come as well).

After raising this question, I worked with my WPI research group to find the answer.  After collecting responses through a survey and performing hypothesis testing (alpha = 0.05), we found that 82% of participants rated themselves as being significantly more concerned about microplastics than their peers while in reality, only a minority of participants expressed low concerns. This insight is concerning---despite people generally being concerned and willing to support reforms, they pessimistically believe others to not share their view. What stands in the way of change is not necessarily just the apathy of people; it is just as much the people's belief in the apathy of others.
